Design Logic

Bamboo scaffolding works because it accepts uncertainty. Poles vary in diameter, surfaces are irregular, and the structure is assembled quickly with tools and judgement rather than precise parts. Yet the system remains stable because the joint is not a single rigid fastener. It is a process: wrapping, tightening, building pressure, and letting friction lock the connection.

The project begins by treating that “manual intelligence” as something that can be translated into rules. The first step is to extract what the knot actually does mechanically: tension becomes radial pressure, radial pressure increases contact, and contact becomes frictional lock. The work reformulates this behaviour into parameters that a 3D-printed component can control.

Design Workflow: Translating manual knot logic into digital parameters.

Joint System

At the centre of the thesis is the node (Box Node), designed as a rigid component that reproduces friction-based locking through geometry rather than straps. The node replaces the manual zipper / lashing action with a mechanical tightening sequence made of two parts: a central hub and a compression cap.

The lock is created by selected zones of pressure, not by uniform clamping everywhere. This matters because bamboo is not perfectly round, and forcing uniform compression would either fail to grip or damage the material.

Physical Components: A set of 3D-printed node components revealing the internal locking geometry and modular interfaces used to connect bamboo members.

Geometry Analysis: Close-ups of the ribbed locking mechanism.

Technical Evolution

Inside the node, ribbed surfaces increase friction and also create tolerance. Instead of assuming a fixed diameter, the node is shaped to accommodate variation and still hold tightly. The rib system increases contact area where grip is needed, while allowing slight adjustment where bamboo diameter changes.

This is the key shift of the project: the joint does not demand perfect material; the geometry is built to accept natural irregularity and still remain readable and repeatable to assemble.

Technical Orthographics: Defining the clamp tolerances.

Method & Construction

This thesis demonstrates a design method that moves cleanly across scales: material behaviour becomes mechanical logic, mechanical logic becomes geometry, geometry becomes a repeatable system, and the system becomes architecture.

Another important decision is that the joint is not hidden. In scaffolding, the tie is visible and the structure is understood through its connections. This project keeps that clarity. The node becomes a spatial element, not only a technical detail.

Physical Prototype: Verifying assembly and structural stability.

From Node to Pavilion

The pavilion is used as the proof that the node system is not only a component, but an architectural language. Instead of a single fixed shape, it is generated through a continuous rule chain:

  • Scenario inputs define the site boundary and entrances.
  • These are translated into an intensity field describing compression or release.
  • The field drives grid density, structural spacing, and roof profile.
  • Voids and clearances are carved for paths and gathering pockets.
  • A roof profile is generated as a response to the same field.
