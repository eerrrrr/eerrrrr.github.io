Site & Context

The project is located in Longtan, a suburb of Nanjing, positioned at the critical intersection between the town and the mountain. The site sits on a former quarry depression with complex contours.

The design treats the complex sloping plots as a determining factor. The building acts as a "mountain pavilion," creating a primary route that links the residential area below to the mountain nature above. It addresses the lack of stadiums and public centers in the region by combining cultural, sports, and tourism functions.

Design Strategy

The massing strategy avoids a single monolithic block. Instead, the building is conceived as a group of units placed diagonally toward the slope. This "Topology and Terrace" approach creates a hierarchy of spaces that separates the street from the mountain while maintaining visual connections.

Interior spaces deepen as the site moves into the hillside, allowing the architecture to feel embedded in the landscape rather than merely sitting on top of it.

Programme Arrangement

Functions are arranged vertically based on their public accessibility. Sports facilities are placed at the bottom level, directly serving the community. The Exhibition Hall sits in the middle, acting as a cultural connector.

The Multimedia and Tourism functions are located at the top, offering controlled access and panoramic views. This arrangement creates a continuous route where visitors enter through a central atrium and ascend through a sequence of courtyards and platforms.

Reciprocal Structure

The project employs a distinctive reciprocal timber beam system. Inspired by Villard de Honnecourt’s planar grillage from the medieval age, the design uses four shorter beams connected within the same plane to span distances longer than the available individual timbers.

This structural logic not only solves the span issue but also becomes an aesthetic driver. The array of wooden beams creates a rhythmic ceiling landscape that guides visitors through the building, reinforcing the spatial hierarchy.

Embedded in the Landscape

The sectional design reveals how the building steps down the quarry slope. Primary structural lines align with contour changes, reducing excessive earthwork. This allows natural light to penetrate deep into the lower levels through skylights and courtyards.

Visual Experience

The diagonal placement of units creates extended sightlines. Visitors moving through the axis experience a contrast between the vertical ascent of the structure and horizontal views towards the town and valley.
