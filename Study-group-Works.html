Purpose of the Study

The aim of the model was not to produce a presentation object, but to read the building through construction. By rebuilding the pavilion at a detailed scale, the team examined how structure, light, and spatial order work together in Ignazio Gardella’s design.

The model allowed close observation of proportional relationships between columns, slabs, and walls, how light enters and moves through the interior, and the transition between indoor and outdoor spaces. It also revealed the clarity of the structural system beneath the architectural form.

Making Process

The model was constructed by breaking the building down into structural layers: base, vertical supports, horizontal slabs, and enclosure elements. This step-by-step assembly helped clarify how the pavilion is held together and how space is defined by structure rather than surface treatment.

Special attention was given to column spacing and alignment, slab thickness and overhangs, sectional depth, and spatial compression. Working at 1:50 scale made construction tolerances visible and required careful control of joints, spacing, and material thickness.

Spatial Reading

Viewing the model at eye level reveals how the pavilion guides movement and frames views. The low horizontal roof planes and slender vertical elements create a calm, ordered interior, while openings and edges establish a continuous dialogue with the exterior.

By moving through the model visually and physically, the team could understand how Gardella uses restraint and repetition to produce spatial richness without formal complexity.

Reflection

This project reinforced the value of physical model-making as a design tool. Reconstructing the pavilion made architectural decisions tangible and exposed the logic behind them. The process strengthened skills in reading structure, understanding proportion, and translating drawings into built form—skills directly relevant to architectural and design practice.

Study Group Works

This project is a 1:50 physical model reconstruction of PAC – Padiglione d’Arte Contemporanea, designed by Ignazio Gardella. The work focuses on understanding the building through making, using model construction as a tool to study proportion, structure, and spatial sequence.
